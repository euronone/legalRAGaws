                              JUDGMENT
------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.  The Plaintiff, M/s. Pinnacle Industries Pvt. Ltd. (hereinafter "Pinnacle"),
    has instituted the present commercial suit against M/s. Zenith Solutions
    Ltd. (hereinafter "Zenith") seeking damages in the sum of Rs. 4,75,00,000/-
    (Rupees Four Crores Seventy-Five Lakhs Only) on account of breach of a
    Supply Agreement dated 1st April, 2021 (hereinafter "the Agreement").

FACTS OF THE CASE:

2.  Pinnacle is engaged in the manufacture and supply of precision automotive
    components. Zenith is an automobile ancillary company that supplies
    assembled modules to major Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs).

3.  On 1st April, 2021, the parties entered into the Agreement whereby
    Pinnacle agreed to supply 50,000 units of high-precision engine
    mounting brackets (Part No. PMB-4421) to Zenith at a unit price of
    Rs. 1,850/- per unit, with delivery to be completed in four quarterly
    installments of 12,500 units each, commencing from 1st July, 2021.

4.  Pinnacle duly supplied the first two installments (25,000 units) within
    the stipulated time. However, on 15th January, 2022, Zenith addressed
    a letter to Pinnacle (Exhibit P-5) purporting to terminate the
    Agreement on grounds of alleged quality deficiency and non-conformance
    with specifications.

5.  Pinnacle contends that the termination was wrongful and without basis.
    The remaining 25,000 units were ready for dispatch, and Pinnacle had
    invested Rs. 1,25,00,000/- in raw materials and tooling for the
    remaining order. As a consequence of the wrongful repudiation, Pinnacle
    claims the following heads of damages:

    (a) Loss of profit on undelivered units:        Rs. 1,87,50,000/-
    (b) Cost of raw materials (wasted):             Rs. 1,25,00,000/-
    (c) Loss of goodwill and business reputation:   Rs.   50,00,000/-
    (d) Interest on blocked capital:                Rs.   12,50,000/-
                                            Total: Rs. 4,75,00,000/-

ARGUMENTS ON BEHALF OF THE PLAINTIFF:

6.  Learned Senior Counsel for the Plaintiff submitted that the termination
    of the Agreement by Zenith was without lawful cause and constitutes a
    breach of contract under Section 73 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872.
    It was argued that when a party to a contract refuses to perform its
    promise, the aggrieved party is entitled to compensation for any loss
    or damage caused thereby.

7.  Reliance was placed on Section 39 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872,
    which provides that when a party to a contract has refused to perform
    or disabled himself from performing his promise in its entirety, the
    promisee may put an end to the contract unless he has signified his
    acquiescence in its continuance.

8.  Reference was made to the decision in ONGC Ltd. v. Saw Pipes Ltd.
    (2003) 5 SCC 705, wherein the Hon'ble Supreme Court held that
    compensation for breach of contract must be reasonable and must
    naturally arise in the usual course of things from such breach.

9.  The Plaintiff produced Quality Inspection Reports (Exhibits P-7 to
    P-12) from an independent NABL-accredited laboratory confirming that
    all supplied units conformed to IS 2062:2011 and the agreed technical
    specifications. Additionally, the Plaintiff relied upon email
    correspondence (Exhibits P-13 to P-18) showing that Zenith had
    accepted and used the first two installments without any complaint.

ARGUMENTS ON BEHALF OF THE DEFENDANT:

10. Learned Counsel for the Defendant submitted that Zenith was entitled
    to terminate the Agreement under Clause 12.3 thereof, which provided
    for termination in the event of non-conformance with quality
    standards. It was argued that internal quality audits of Zenith
    (Exhibit D-2) revealed micro-fractures in 3% of the supplied brackets,
    exceeding the acceptable defect rate of 0.5%.

11. It was further contended that under Section 16 of the Sale of Goods
    Act, 1930, there is an implied condition as to merchantability, and
    the goods supplied failed to meet this condition. Reliance was placed
    on Steel Authority of India Ltd. v. Gupta Brother Steel Tubes Ltd.
    (2009) 10 SCC 63.

12. The Defendant also argued that the damages claimed are excessive,
    speculative, and do not satisfy the test of remoteness laid down in
    Hadley v. Baxendale (1854) 9 Ex. 341, as adopted in Indian
    jurisprudence through Murlidhar Chiranjilal v. Harishchandra
    Dwarkadas, AIR 1962 SC 366.

EVIDENCE AND ANALYSIS:

13. This Court has carefully examined the documentary and oral evidence
    on record. The Quality Inspection Reports from the NABL-accredited
    laboratory (Exhibits P-7 to P-12) are contemporaneous and credible
    documents. They conclusively establish that the brackets supplied by
    Pinnacle were within the specified tolerance levels.

14. The internal audit report of Zenith (Exhibit D-2) was prepared
    unilaterally without affording Pinnacle an opportunity of joint
    inspection as mandated under Clause 8.2 of the Agreement. This
    non-compliance with the contractual procedure renders the audit
    report unreliable. Reference is made to Indian Oil Corporation Ltd.
    v. M/s. Royal Petroleum (2018) SCC OnLine Bom 745.

15. Furthermore, this Court notes that under Section 41 of the Indian
    Contract Act, 1872, when a party accepts performance in part, and
    later seeks to repudiate the entire contract, the burden lies on
    the repudiating party to establish that the breach goes to the root
    of the contract. Zenith accepted and consumed 25,000 units without
    protest, which strongly negates their subsequent claim of quality
    deficiency.

16. As regards the quantum of damages, this Court applies the principle
    under Section 73 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872. The loss of
    profit on undelivered units and the cost of raw materials are direct
    and natural consequences of the breach and are adequately proved.
    However, the claims for loss of goodwill and interest on blocked
    capital are speculative and not supported by sufficient evidence.

JUDGMENT AND ORDER:

17. In view of the foregoing analysis, this Court holds that:

    (a) The termination of the Agreement dated 1st April, 2021, by the
        Defendant was wrongful and constitutes a breach of contract.

    (b) The Defendant is liable to compensate the Plaintiff for damages
        naturally arising from the breach.

18. The Defendant is directed to pay to the Plaintiff the following:

    (i)   Loss of profit on undelivered goods:  Rs. 1,87,50,000/-
    (ii)  Cost of wasted raw materials:         Rs. 1,25,00,000/-
                                        Total: Rs. 3,12,50,000/-

    (iii) Interest at the rate of 9% per annum on the aforesaid sum from
          the date of breach (15th January, 2022) until the date of
          realization.

    (iv)  The claim for loss of goodwill (Rs. 50,00,000/-) and interest
          on blocked capital (Rs. 12,50,000/-) is disallowed for want of
          sufficient evidence.

    (v)   Costs of the suit assessed at Rs. 3,00,000/- (Rupees Three
          Lakhs Only) shall be borne by the Defendant.

19. The Defendant shall make the payment within 90 days from the date of
    this judgment, failing which the Plaintiff shall be entitled to
    execute the decree in accordance with law.

20. Decree sheet to be drawn up accordingly.
