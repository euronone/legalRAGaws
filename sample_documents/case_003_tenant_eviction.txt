                               ORDER
------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.  The Petitioner/Landlord has filed the present petition under Section
    10 of the Tamil Nadu Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1960
    (hereinafter referred to as "the Act") seeking eviction of the
    Respondent/Tenant from the ground floor premises bearing Door No.
    28/A, Cathedral Road, Gopalapuram, Chennai - 600086, admeasuring
    approximately 1,200 square feet (hereinafter referred to as "the
    demised premises").

FACTS OF THE CASE:

2.  The Petitioner is the absolute owner of the building situated at No.
    28, Cathedral Road, Gopalapuram, Chennai, which comprises a ground
    floor and two upper floors. The ground floor was let out to the
    Respondent under a registered lease deed dated 5th March, 2015
    (Exhibit P-1) for a period of five years at a monthly rent of Rs.
    35,000/- (Rupees Thirty-Five Thousand Only), with an annual
    enhancement of 5%.

3.  The lease expired on 4th March, 2020. The Respondent continued in
    occupation as a statutory tenant. The current fair rent as applicable
    is Rs. 44,677/- per month.

4.  The Petitioner seeks eviction on the following grounds:

    (a) Non-payment of rent: The Respondent has defaulted in payment of
        rent for a continuous period of 18 months from January 2022 to
        June 2023, resulting in arrears amounting to Rs. 8,04,186/-
        (Rupees Eight Lakhs Four Thousand One Hundred and Eighty-Six
        Only). [Ground under Section 10(2)(i) of the Act]

    (b) Bona fide requirement: The Petitioner requires the demised
        premises for his own occupation as he intends to establish a
        medical clinic, being a registered medical practitioner
        (Registration No. TN/MC/42517). The Petitioner does not own any
        other suitable premises in the city of Chennai. [Ground under
        Section 10(2)(vi) of the Act]

    (c) Material alteration: The Respondent has, without the written
        consent of the Petitioner, made structural alterations to the
        premises by constructing a mezzanine floor and converting a
        portion of the residential premises into a commercial
        establishment. [Ground under Section 10(2)(ii)(b) of the Act]

ARGUMENTS ON BEHALF OF THE PETITIONER:

5.  Learned Counsel for the Petitioner submitted that the Respondent has
    been a habitual defaulter. Despite receiving a registered notice
    under Section 106 of the Transfer of Property Act, 1882, dated 10th
    July, 2022 (Exhibit P-3), demanding payment of arrears and delivery
    of vacant possession, the Respondent failed to comply. It was
    submitted that the arrears stood at Rs. 8,04,186/- as on the date
    of filing of the petition.

6.  On the ground of bona fide requirement, the Petitioner produced his
    medical registration certificate (Exhibit P-5) and a business plan
    for establishing a general medical clinic. It was argued that under
    Section 10(2)(vi) of the Act, a landlord's bona fide requirement for
    own occupation is a valid ground for eviction. Reliance was placed on
    the decision in S. Rajagopal v. S.T. Veerasamy, AIR 1980 Mad 10,
    and the Supreme Court's ruling in Bega Begum v. Abdul Ahad Khan,
    (1979) 1 SCC 273.

7.  Regarding the material alteration, the Petitioner relied upon the
    report of the Municipal Building Inspector (Exhibit P-8) and
    photographs (Exhibits P-9 to P-14) establishing the construction
    of an unauthorized mezzanine floor. It was submitted that under
    Section 10(2)(ii)(b) of the Act, material alteration without the
    landlord's written consent is an independent ground for eviction.

ARGUMENTS ON BEHALF OF THE RESPONDENT:

8.  Learned Counsel for the Respondent contended that the Respondent had
    tendered rent through money orders which were returned by the
    Petitioner. In support, the Respondent produced postal receipts
    (Exhibits R-1 to R-4) for the months of January 2022 to April 2022.
    It was argued that the Petitioner deliberately refused to accept rent
    in order to manufacture a ground for eviction.

9.  On the question of bona fide requirement, it was submitted that the
    Petitioner already owns a property at No. 72, T. Nagar, Chennai,
    where he could establish his clinic. The Respondent produced a
    property tax receipt (Exhibit R-6) showing the Petitioner's ownership
    of the T. Nagar property.

10. As regards the alleged material alteration, the Respondent contended
    that the modifications were minor in nature and were carried out with
    the oral permission of the Petitioner. However, no documentary
    evidence of such oral consent was produced.

ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS:

Ground (a) - Non-payment of rent:

11. Upon examination of the evidence, this Court finds that the Respondent
    produced postal receipts for money orders only for the period January
    to April 2022 (four months). No evidence of tender of rent was
    produced for the period May 2022 to June 2023 (fourteen months). Even
    accepting the Respondent's contention for the first four months, a
    default of fourteen months remains unexplained.

12. Under Section 10(2)(i) of the Act, wilful default in payment of rent
    is a ground for eviction. The Respondent failed to avail the remedy
    under Section 11(3) of the Act, which permits a tenant to deposit
    arrears in Court to avoid eviction. No such deposit was made.
    Reference is made to M/s. Precision Fasteners Ltd. v. Dattaram,
    (2002) 6 SCC 234.

13. This ground is established.

Ground (b) - Bona fide requirement:

14. The T. Nagar property (Exhibit R-6) is a residential apartment of
    600 square feet, which is manifestly unsuitable for establishing a
    medical clinic. The Petitioner has satisfactorily demonstrated that
    he requires the demised premises (1,200 square feet, ground floor,
    commercially located on Cathedral Road) for his genuine professional
    needs. The requirement is not a mere desire but a genuine necessity.
    Reference is made to Smt. Prativa Devi v. T.V. Krishnamoorthy,
    (1996) 5 SCC 353, wherein the Hon'ble Supreme Court held that the
    landlord's requirement need not be shown to be a matter of life
    and death; it suffices that the requirement is genuine and honest.

15. This ground is established.

Ground (c) - Material alteration:

16. The Municipal Building Inspector's report (Exhibit P-8) and the
    photographic evidence conclusively establish that a mezzanine floor
    was constructed without permission. The Respondent's claim of oral
    permission is unsupported and is rejected. This constitutes material
    alteration within the meaning of Section 10(2)(ii)(b) of the Act.

17. This ground is established.

ORDER:

18. In view of the findings on all three grounds, this Court passes the
    following order:

    (a) The Respondent/Tenant is hereby directed to vacate and deliver
        peaceful vacant possession of the demised premises, being Door
        No. 28/A, Ground Floor, Cathedral Road, Gopalapuram, Chennai -
        600086, to the Petitioner/Landlord within a period of THREE
        MONTHS from the date of this order.

    (b) The Respondent is directed to pay the arrears of rent amounting
        to Rs. 8,04,186/- together with mesne profits at the rate of
        Rs. 44,677/- per month from July 2023 until the date of actual
        vacation, within one month from the date of this order.

    (c) The Respondent shall restore the premises to its original
        condition by removing the unauthorized mezzanine construction
        before handing over possession, at his own cost.

    (d) In the event of failure to vacate within the stipulated period,
        the Petitioner shall be at liberty to seek execution of this
        order through the Executing Court.

    (e) Costs assessed at Rs. 25,000/- (Rupees Twenty-Five Thousand
        Only) to be paid by the Respondent.
