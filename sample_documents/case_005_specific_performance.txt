                         JUDGMENT AND DECREE
------------------------------------------------------------------------

1.  The Plaintiff has instituted the present suit seeking a decree for
    specific performance of an Agreement to Sell dated 12th August, 2021,
    executed by Defendant No. 1 in favour of the Plaintiff in respect of
    immovable property bearing Khata No. 142/3, situated at Sy. No. 45/2,
    Kengeri Hobli, Bengaluru South Taluk, admeasuring 2,400 square feet
    (hereinafter referred to as the "Schedule Property"), and for a
    permanent injunction restraining the Defendants from alienating the
    Schedule Property to any third party.

FACTS OF THE CASE:

2.  By an Agreement to Sell dated 12th August, 2021 (Exhibit P-1),
    Defendant No. 1, claiming to be the absolute owner of the Schedule
    Property, agreed to sell the same to the Plaintiff for a total sale
    consideration of Rs. 96,00,000/- (Rupees Ninety-Six Lakhs Only).

3.  The terms of the Agreement provided as follows:
    (a) Earnest money of Rs. 15,00,000/- (Rupees Fifteen Lakhs Only)
        was paid by the Plaintiff to Defendant No. 1 at the time of
        execution of the Agreement, by way of demand draft No. 452178
        dated 12th August, 2021, drawn on State Bank of India, Jayanagar
        Branch (acknowledged in the Agreement, Exhibit P-1, Clause 3).
    (b) The balance consideration of Rs. 81,00,000/- was to be paid at
        the time of registration of the Sale Deed.
    (c) The sale deed was to be executed and registered within six months
        from the date of the Agreement, i.e., on or before 11th February,
        2022.
    (d) Defendant No. 1 undertook to obtain all necessary clearances,
        including an encumbrance certificate and Khata transfer.

4.  The Plaintiff avers that she was at all times ready and willing to
    perform her part of the Agreement. She obtained a home loan sanction
    letter dated 20th January, 2022 (Exhibit P-3) from Canara Bank,
    Jayanagar Branch, for Rs. 70,00,000/- and arranged the remaining
    funds through personal savings.

5.  On 5th February, 2022, one week before the deadline, the Plaintiff's
    advocate addressed a letter (Exhibit P-4) to Defendant No. 1
    requesting him to fix the date and time for execution and registration
    of the sale deed. Defendant No. 1 failed to respond.

6.  On 15th February, 2022, the Plaintiff came to learn that Defendant
    No. 1 had entered into negotiations with a third party, Shri Ganesh
    Rao, for the sale of the same Schedule Property at a higher price of
    Rs. 1,20,00,000/-. The Plaintiff immediately caused a legal notice
    dated 18th February, 2022 (Exhibit P-5) to be served upon the
    Defendants calling upon Defendant No. 1 to honour the Agreement.

7.  Defendant No. 1 replied through his advocate on 5th March, 2022
    (Exhibit P-6), stating that the Agreement stood cancelled as
    Defendant No. 2 (his wife) had not consented to the sale, and that
    the property was joint property requiring her consent under Section
    6 of the Hindu Succession Act, 1956.

ARGUMENTS ON BEHALF OF THE PLAINTIFF:

8.  Learned Senior Counsel for the Plaintiff submitted that the Plaintiff
    has satisfied all the conditions for grant of specific performance
    under Section 16 of the Specific Relief Act, 1963 (as amended in
    2018). He argued that:

    (a) The Agreement to Sell (Exhibit P-1) is a valid, enforceable
        contract supported by consideration.
    (b) The Plaintiff was at all material times ready and willing to
        perform her obligations under the Agreement. She had arranged
        the entire sale consideration.
    (c) The Defendants' refusal to perform is without lawful justification.

9.  Regarding the amendment to Section 14(1) of the Specific Relief Act,
    1963, by the Specific Relief (Amendment) Act, 2018, Senior Counsel
    submitted that specific performance is now to be granted as a general
    rule, and the discretionary nature of the relief has been
    substantially curtailed. The amended Section 14 provides that
    specific performance of a contract shall be enforced by the Court
    subject only to the provisions of sub-sections (2) to (4), replacing
    the earlier regime of judicial discretion under the erstwhile Section
    20.

10. Reliance was placed on the following precedents:
    (a) Sushil Kumar Jain v. Manoj Kumar (2021) SCC OnLine SC 927,
        wherein the Hon'ble Supreme Court reiterated that after the 2018
        amendment, specific performance is a matter of right and not
        discretion.
    (b) P. D'Souza v. Shondrilo Naidu (2004) 6 SCC 649, wherein it was
        held that the willingness to perform must be established
        throughout and not merely at the date of the contract.
    (c) Parakunnan Veetil Joseph's Son Mathew v. Nedumbara Kuruvila's
        Son (1987) 1 SCC 340, wherein the Supreme Court held that time
        is not of the essence in contracts for sale of immovable
        property unless expressly stipulated.

11. On the question of Defendant No. 2's consent, it was submitted that
    the title documents (Exhibits P-7 and P-8), including the original
    Sale Deed dated 3rd May, 2010, and the Khata Certificate, record
    Defendant No. 1 as the sole owner. The property was purchased by
    Defendant No. 1 from his self-acquired funds and is not joint
    family property.

ARGUMENTS ON BEHALF OF THE DEFENDANTS:

12. Learned Counsel for the Defendants raised the following contentions:

    (a) The Plaintiff failed to tender the balance consideration within
        the stipulated time, and therefore the Agreement stands
        terminated by efflux of time.
    (b) Defendant No. 2, being the wife of Defendant No. 1, has a
        right of residence under Section 17(1) of the Protection of
        Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005, and any sale without
        her consent would be prejudicial to her interests.
    (c) The specific performance cannot be granted as the Plaintiff has
        not come to the Court with clean hands and delayed filing the
        suit.

13. Reliance was placed on K. Narendra v. Riviera Apartments (1999) 5
    SCC 694, wherein it was held that a party seeking specific
    performance must demonstrate continuous readiness and willingness.

ANALYSIS AND FINDINGS:

14. On the question of readiness and willingness, this Court is satisfied
    that the Plaintiff has amply demonstrated her readiness to perform.
    The home loan sanction letter (Exhibit P-3), the advocate's letter
    requesting fixation of date (Exhibit P-4), and the legal notice
    (Exhibit P-5) collectively establish continuous readiness.

15. Regarding the contention that time was of the essence, this Court
    notes that the Agreement (Exhibit P-1) does not contain any clause
    making time the essence of the contract. Under Section 55 of the
    Indian Contract Act, 1872, read with the established principle in
    Chand Rani v. Kamal Rani (1993) 1 SCC 519, in contracts for sale
    of immovable property, time is generally not of the essence unless
    specifically made so.

16. The objection regarding Defendant No. 2's consent is without merit.
    The Schedule Property is recorded solely in the name of Defendant
    No. 1, and the Defendants have produced no evidence to show that
    it is joint family property or that Defendant No. 2 holds any
    proprietary interest therein. The right of residence under the
    Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005, is a personal
    right against the husband and does not create a proprietary interest
    that can defeat a contractual obligation to a third party.

17. As to the allegation of unclean hands, no cogent evidence of fraud,
    misrepresentation, or unconscionable conduct on the part of the
    Plaintiff has been produced. The suit was filed within the period
    of limitation prescribed under Article 54 of the Limitation Act,
    1963 (three years from the date fixed for performance).

18. This Court finds that the Agreement to Sell dated 12th August, 2021,
    is valid and enforceable, and the Defendants have committed breach
    thereof without lawful justification. The Plaintiff is entitled to
    specific performance under the Specific Relief Act, 1963.

DECREE:

19. In view of the above findings, the following decree is passed:

    (a) The suit is decreed in favour of the Plaintiff.

    (b) A decree for specific performance of the Agreement to Sell
        dated 12th August, 2021 (Exhibit P-1) is hereby passed,
        directing the Defendants to execute and register the Sale Deed
        in respect of the Schedule Property in favour of the Plaintiff,
        upon receipt of the balance consideration of Rs. 81,00,000/-
        (Rupees Eighty-One Lakhs Only), within a period of 60 days from
        the date of this decree.

    (c) The Plaintiff shall deposit the balance consideration of
        Rs. 81,00,000/- in this Court within 30 days from the date of
        this decree.

    (d) In the event the Defendants fail to execute and register the
        Sale Deed within the stipulated period, the decree shall be
        executed through the Court under Order XXI of the Code of Civil
        Procedure, 1908, and the Court shall execute the Sale Deed on
        behalf of the Defendants in accordance with Section 21(1) of
        the Specific Relief Act, 1963.

    (e) The Defendants are permanently restrained from alienating,
        encumbering, or creating any third-party interest in the
        Schedule Property pending execution of this decree.

    (f) The Defendants shall bear the costs of this suit, assessed at
        Rs. 1,50,000/- (Rupees One Lakh Fifty Thousand Only).
